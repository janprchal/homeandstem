A balcony herb garden is one of the lowest-risk ways to start growing your own food. This
takes about **30 minutes** to set up, costs around **$65** in supplies, and is firmly
**beginner**-friendly — no garden bed or yard required.

<WhatYouNeedList variant="full" items={frontmatter.essentials} />

<StepBlock number={1} title="Choose a container with real drainage">
Herbs hate sitting in wet soil. A rectangular planter with multiple drainage holes fits 3–4
herb starts side by side, which is the realistic minimum for a useful starter garden rather
than one pot per herb.
</StepBlock>

<StepBlock number={2} title="Fill with all-purpose potting mix, not garden soil">
Garden soil compacts in containers and drains poorly. An all-purpose potting mix formulated
for container plants stays loose and feeds the plant for months on its own.
</StepBlock>

<StepBlock number={3} title="Plant your herbs, keeping mint separate">
Basil, parsley, thyme, and cilantro can share a container. Mint spreads aggressively and will
crowd out everything else — always give it its own pot.
</StepBlock>

<StepBlock number={4} title="Place it where it gets at least 6 hours of sun">
Most culinary herbs want a half-day of direct sun. A south- or west-facing balcony rail is
usually the brightest spot — rotate the container weekly if light comes from one side only.
</StepBlock>

## Tips and Common Mistakes to Avoid

- Water when the top inch of soil is dry, not on a fixed schedule — balcony heat changes how
  fast containers dry out.
- Harvest by snipping the outer stems, not the center growth point, so the plant keeps
  producing.
- Skip fertilizer for the first few weeks if you used a fresh potting mix — it already has a
  feed charge built in.

<CalloutBox variant="tip" title="Keep mint contained">
  Mint will take over an entire container within a season if planted with other herbs. Keep
  it in its own pot, even a small one, and you'll save the rest of your herbs.
</CalloutBox>
