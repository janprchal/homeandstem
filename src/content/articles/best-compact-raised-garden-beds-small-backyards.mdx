A raised bed solves the two biggest small-backyard gardening problems at once: poor native
soil and limited flat ground. You don't need a sprawling plot — a single compact kit tucked
against a fence or patio edge is enough to grow real vegetables. Here's what to check before
buying, and three kits worth the money.

## What to Look for in a Raised Garden Bed

### Material: galvanized metal, cedar, or composite

Galvanized steel is the lightest and cheapest to ship, with a long rust-resistant life.
Cedar costs more but looks more like furniture and naturally resists rot. Both outlast
cheap pine or plastic kits.

### Height and bottomless vs. closed-bottom design

Taller beds (16"+) mean less bending and better root depth for vegetables like carrots, but
also need more soil to fill. Bottomless designs drain better and let roots reach native
soil below.

### Footprint relative to your actual space

Measure the spot before ordering — a bed that looks compact in product photos can still eat
most of a small patio. Vertical, tiered designs trade footprint for height when ground
space is the real constraint.

### Assembly

Toolless panel kits go together in minutes; cedar box kits are usually still tool-free but
take a little longer. Either is faster than building one from scratch.
